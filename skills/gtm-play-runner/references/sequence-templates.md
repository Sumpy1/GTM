# Sequence Templates

Most plays are sequences, not one-shots. The plays in `plays/` give you the play-specific cadence; this file gives you the channel-specific patterns to fill in when a play needs a touch in a channel beyond email.

---

## Multi-channel cadence patterns

### The "warm fast, break clean" pattern (cold-ish, time-sensitive signals)

Best for: funding rounds, site visits, news signals, abandoned forms.

- **Day 0:** Email — the trigger-specific opener.
- **Day 2-3:** Email follow-up with one piece of new value (case study, comparison, one-liner answer).
- **Day 5-7:** LinkedIn touch — connect or DM with a one-line note. No pitch.
- **Day 10-14:** Final email — "wrapping up unless I hear back."
- **Stop.** Set a quarterly reminder for re-engage.

Total touches: 4. Total duration: ~2 weeks. Stop before it becomes harassment.

### The "patient warm" pattern (relationship-based plays)

Best for: external moves (champion at new company), executive reach, ABM accounts.

- **Day 0:** Light intro email or LinkedIn DM. No business.
- **Day 7-14:** Adjacent touch on different channel. Still no business.
- **Day 30-45:** Light check-in. "Settling in?"
- **Day 60:** First substantive ask, opened gently.
- **Day 90:** Break or shift to quarterly cadence.

Total touches: 4-5. Total duration: ~3 months. The relationship is the asset.

### The "confirm and execute" pattern (active deals)

Best for: stalled opportunities, e-sign follow-up, meeting prep.

- **Day 0:** Direct email naming the situation and asking a specific question.
- **Day 3-5:** Phone call.
- **Day 7-10:** Final email with escalation path or honest "is this real?" framing.
- **Day 14:** Decision — kill, escalate, or pause.

Total touches: 3-4. Total duration: ~2 weeks. The deal is either real or it isn't.

## Email patterns

### The 4-line opener
Best for cold signals. Buyers read the first 4 lines on mobile before deciding.

```
[Specific trigger or observation — 1 sentence.]

[Why it matters / what most people in their situation are dealing with — 1 sentence.]

[Specific value you offer that addresses it — 1 sentence.]

[Soft ask — 1 sentence.]
```

Example:
> Saw the Series B close last week. Most engineering leaders we work with hit a tooling-vs-headcount gap about 60-90 days post-funding. We help teams at [comparable company] close that gap in their first quarter. Worth 20 minutes to compare notes?

### The reply-to-self follow-up
A clean way to follow up without writing a new email. Reply to your own original email with one line of new context.

```
Quick follow-up on this — [one piece of new value or context, e.g., "we just published a case study on exactly this scenario at [comparable company]"]. Worth a look?
```

### The "honest read" close
For deals that have gone quiet. The numbered framing forces a decision.

```
Want to give an honest read. From what I'm seeing — [specific signals: gaps in next steps, lack of stakeholder engagement, etc.] — this doesn't feel like it's going to close in [timeframe]. I'd rather hear I'm wrong than keep pushing on something that isn't going to land.

Two questions:
1. Am I reading this right, or am I missing something?
2. If I'm right, what's the most useful path — pause until [trigger], close-lost, or something else?
```

## LinkedIn patterns

### Connection request with context (no pitch)
```
Saw [specific thing — the move, the post, the news]. Reaching out to stay in touch — no pitch, just like to be connected to [function] leaders working on [topic].
```

### DM after meaningful post engagement
```
Your post on [topic] resonated — particularly the point about [specific thing]. Most [function] leaders I talk to are dealing with the same thing. Curious if you're seeing it the same way.
```

### Voice note (use sparingly)
60 seconds, max. Open with the recipient's name, name the specific reason for reaching out, name the specific value, leave a one-line ask. Don't pitch.

LinkedIn voice notes work because they're rare. Send them more than once a quarter to the same person and they lose effect.

## Phone patterns

### Cold call opener (after a real signal)
```
Hi [name], this is [your name] from [company]. The reason for the call: [specific signal — e.g., "saw your form come in 5 minutes ago," "saw the Series B announcement last week"]. Got 2 minutes for one question?
```

The "got 2 minutes for one question?" is the key move. Most cold calls fail because they ask for a meeting in the first 30 seconds. Asking for permission to keep talking converts dramatically better.

### The voicemail
```
Hi [name], [your name] from [company]. Calling about [specific reason — name the signal]. I'll send you an email in the next minute with the same question — easier to read than to call back. Talk soon.
```

The voicemail's job is to make the email expected. People who got a voicemail open the email at much higher rates than people who didn't.

### Follow-up call after an unanswered email
```
Hi [name], following up on the email I sent [time]. The short version: [one-sentence summary of the email]. Got 30 seconds?
```

## Chat / on-site patterns

### Chat opener after high-intent page visit
```
Hey, saw you spent some time on [page]. Most folks looking at that page have one of two questions — [Q1] or [Q2]. Either of those, or something different?
```

### Chat after abandoned form
```
Saw you started the form and stepped away. Anything I can help with directly here?
```

## Physical mail patterns

### The book + handwritten note
Send a book on their problem area. Handwritten note inside the cover.

```
[Name],

[One sentence about why this book — what specific chapter / idea you think they'll find useful given their work.]

If [specific topic] is something you're actively working on, my team would love to compare notes.

— [Your first name]
[Phone] / [Email]
```

Then 3 days after expected delivery: an email — "did the book arrive? Wanted to make sure it didn't get caught in the mailroom."

### What not to send
Branded swag (mugs, t-shirts, fidget toys) goes in the trash. The unit economics are terrible and the recipient doesn't remember it. Send something they'll use or read.

## Ad patterns (paid retargeting)

### Account-targeted ad after site visit
Headline: A specific question about their problem ("Still evaluating [tool category]?")
Body: One specific value prop, no marketing speak.
CTA: Either "talk to sales" or "watch a 5-minute demo." Not "learn more."

### Account-targeted ad pre-event
Headline: "Going to [event]?"
Body: One reason to meet at the event.
CTA: "Book a slot" with a calendar link.

## Sequence design principles

1. **Channels should rotate, not stack.** Email → LinkedIn → email → call. Not email → email → email.
2. **Each touch should add value or new context.** "Just following up" is not a touch. The reply-to-self pattern only works if you add something new.
3. **Spacing should breathe.** Days, not hours. The exception: form fills (5 minutes) and trial signups (24 hours).
4. **The last touch should be a clean break.** Not "circling back one more time" — "wrapping this up unless I hear back." Make the close visible.
5. **Stop conditions should be explicit.** The rep should know in advance what triggers the disengagement, not figure it out emotionally after touch 7.
