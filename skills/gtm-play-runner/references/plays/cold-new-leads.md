# Cold / New Leads — 21 Plays

The hardest part of cold outreach isn't writing the email. It's having a real reason to send it. Every play in this file is built around a specific signal that gives the rep a non-spammy reason to show up. If you don't have the signal, you don't have the play.

A senior operator's hierarchy of cold signals, best to worst:

1. **Funding rounds** (specific budget event, public, fresh, time-bound)
2. **Internal moves / promotions** (new buyer, new mandate, fresh budget authority)
3. **Competitive tech changes** (added or removed a competitor — they're shopping)
4. **Site visits with intent** (real person, real action, fresh)
5. **Review site activity** (active evaluation, but weaker because vendor-supplied)
6. **News and PR** (good context, weak as a sole trigger)
7. **Lookalike/ICP lists** (just a list — needs a layered signal to be useful)
8. **Generic intent feeds** (mostly noise; layer with another signal or skip)

Run plays in roughly this order of priority when multiple apply.

## Funding Round Signal

**Window:** ~10-14 days from announcement. Dies fast — every other vendor is calling them too.
**Lead channel:** Email, then call.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** Funded companies have new budget, new mandates, and a quarter or two of urgency to deploy capital. The CEO/CFO is being asked "what are you doing with the money" weekly.

**Who to target:** Don't email the CEO unless your deal size is huge. Target the function the round will fund — engineering for infra/devtools, RevOps for sales tools, marketing for martech. Series B+ is the sweet spot; Seed/A is too early for most enterprise products.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Series B + the next two quarters
>
> Saw the [Series X] news — congratulations. Most of the [function] leaders we work with hit the same constraint about 60-90 days post-raise: hiring catches up to budget but tooling doesn't, and the team starts losing the speed advantage the round was supposed to buy.
>
> We help [function] teams at [comparable funded company] solve [specific problem]. Worth 20 minutes to compare notes on what's working in their environment?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 4: LinkedIn connect with a one-line note referencing a specific portfolio fact.
- Day 8: Forward-style email — "circling back on this in case it got buried."
- Day 14: Break. Set a 90-day reminder to check back when the post-funding chaos settles.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 touches in 14 days. Funding signal has decayed.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't lead with congratulations as the only hook. Every vendor sends that email. Lead with the specific operational problem the round creates.

## Contact Internal Moves

**Window:** First 90 days of new role. Best in days 30-60 — past onboarding, before they've committed to a stack.
**Lead channel:** Email, then LinkedIn.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** New executives in their first 90 days are evaluating tooling and looking for early wins. They have political capital to spend and want to make their mark. They are unusually receptive to new conversations — and unusually willing to rip out incumbent tools.

**Who to target:** The promoted person, not their team. The ICP fit shifts: a VP of Sales who just got promoted to CRO is a different buyer than they were last month.

**First touch:**

> Subject: New role — first 90 days
>
> Saw the move to [new title]. Congrats.
>
> Reaching out because most [new role] leaders I talk to in their first quarter say the same thing: they inherit a tooling stack that was built for the prior leader's priorities. Worth half an hour to talk through what we see [function] teams change in the first six months? Happy to share what's working at [comparable company] without a pitch.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: LinkedIn connect, no pitch — just "good to see you in the new seat."
- Day 10: Forward the original email with one new piece of context (a relevant case study, a specific metric).
- Day 21: Break. Reminder to check at day 60-75 for a second window.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 touches. They're swamped. Try again at the 90-day mark.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't pitch the existing buyer's stack as broken. The new leader probably hired some of the people who built it. Frame as "what's the next thing" not "what's wrong with the current thing."

## Contact External Moves

**Window:** First 30-60 days of new role. Earlier than internal moves because external moves come with pre-existing context — your champion already trusts you.
**Lead channel:** Email, warmly written.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** A former champion at a new company is the single highest-converting cold signal in B2B sales. They already know the value of your product. They're choosing their stack. The cost of acquisition is near-zero.

**Who to target:** The mover. Don't go around them.

**First touch:**

> Subject: New chapter
>
> Saw the new role at [new company] — congrats, it's a great fit for the work you did at [old company].
>
> No pitch here, just wanted to say: when you're ready to think about the [tool category] stack at [new company], you know where to find us. Happy to bring my team in for a working session whenever it's useful — or to stay out of the way if you're handling it internally.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 7: LinkedIn message, friendly, no business.
- Day 30: Light check-in email — "settling in?"
- Day 60: If still nothing, drop a relevant resource (case study, benchmark) and ask one open-ended question about their priorities.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 friendly touches. They may be in a non-buying role or may not own the decision. Stay warm but stop initiating.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't immediately pitch a renewal-style discount or "previous customer" pricing. It cheapens the relationship. They moved to make their mark, not to recreate their last setup.

## News Signals (General / Opportunity / Financial)

**Window:** 7-10 days from the news.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** News gives you a non-generic opener. The risk is that everyone is sending news-based emails now and they all blur together.

**Who to target:** The function the news affects. M&A news → CFO/COO. Product launch → product/engineering leadership. Layoffs → HR leadership only with extreme care, and often not at all.

**First touch:**

> Subject: [Specific news event]
>
> Saw the [acquisition / launch / earnings / report]. The piece that stood out: [one specific detail, not a paraphrase of the headline].
>
> If [specific operational implication] is on your plate, we've helped [comparable company] with [specific outcome] in similar moments. Want me to send the one-page version, or is now not the right time?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 4: Reply to your own email with the one-pager attached.
- Day 10: LinkedIn note.
- Day 14: Break.

**Stop condition:** Two touches with no response. Move on; the news has already aged.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't congratulate them on bad news. Don't try to use layoffs, lawsuits, or executive departures as a "warm" opener — it reads as opportunistic and burns the account permanently. If the news is negative, skip the play.

## Review Site Intent

**Window:** 14-21 days. Reviewers are actively evaluating; that window closes when they pick a vendor.
**Lead channel:** Email + LinkedIn ad retargeting.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Someone who reviewed a competitor on G2/Capterra/TrustRadius is in the market right now. The review itself often signals what they like and dislike about the incumbent — read it.

**Who to target:** The reviewer if you can identify them, the function leader if you can't.

**First touch:**

> Subject: [Competitor] review
>
> Saw your review of [competitor]. The line about [specific thing they criticized] is something we hear a lot — [your product] handles it differently because [one-sentence reason].
>
> Worth a 20-minute side-by-side? I can send you the technical comparison directly if you'd rather not take a call.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 4: Send the comparison doc whether they responded or not.
- Day 9: LinkedIn touch.
- Day 14: Break.

**Stop condition:** If they don't respond by day 14, they probably resigned with the incumbent or picked a different alternative. Move on.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't disparage the competitor. The reviewer chose them once and may again. Frame your differentiation as "different approach" not "they're bad."

## Competitive Tech Added / Uninstall

**Window:** Added → 30-60 days (they're in adoption pain). Uninstall → 14 days (they're actively shopping).
**Lead channel:** Email, then call.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate (Added) / Expert (Uninstall — needs technographic data).
**Impact:** High (Added), Medium (Uninstall).

**Why it works:** Tech installs/uninstalls are the strongest "they are shopping right now" signal in B2B. Added = they're in honeymoon and starting to find limits. Uninstall = they just had a bad experience and are looking.

**Who to target:** The function that owns the tool, not the buyer who signed the contract.

**First touch (Added):**

> Subject: 60 days into [competitor]
>
> Most [function] teams hit the same wall about 60 days into [competitor]: [specific limitation]. We built [your product] to handle that case differently — happy to walk through the architecture if it's relevant.

**First touch (Uninstall):**

> Subject: After [competitor]
>
> Noticed [competitor] is no longer part of your stack. If you're evaluating what to replace it with, the things teams typically find missing are [X] and [Y]. Want me to send a short comparison of how we handle both?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: Send the technical resource you offered.
- Day 10: LinkedIn touch.
- Day 14: Break.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 touches. They've either fixed the issue or chosen another vendor.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't out them on the data source. Saying "I saw your tech stack data shows..." sounds creepy and makes the rep look like they're using surveillance tools. Reference the situation, not the data point.

## Site Visit Targeting

**Window:** 24-72 hours. Site visit signals decay in hours, not days.
**Lead channel:** Email + LinkedIn ads simultaneously.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Someone visited your pricing page or solution page. They're researching. The window where you're top-of-mind is measured in days.

**Who to target:** If you have person-level data, the visitor. If only account-level, the closest fit by ICP role at that account.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Quick question about [specific page topic]
>
> Saw some interest from [company] in [specific topic / page]. No assumption that you were the one looking, but if [topic] is on your radar, two questions usually come up at this stage: [Q1] and [Q2]. Happy to answer either by email or take 15 minutes if it's easier.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 2: LinkedIn ad retargeting goes live to that account.
- Day 3: Email follow-up with one-line answer to one of the questions.
- Day 7: Break.

**Stop condition:** No response in 7 days. The signal is cold.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't guess at what they were looking for and pitch it. Open it as a question, not an assumption.

## Lookalike ICP Targeting

**Window:** N/A — this is a list, not a signal. Needs a layered signal to become a play.
**Lead channel:** Multi-channel (email + LinkedIn ads + organic social).
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Lookalike lists give you a higher hit rate than blind cold. But they're not a signal — they're a filter. A senior operator never runs a lookalike list as a standalone cold motion. They run it through ads and warm-up first, then layer a real signal on top before any 1:1 outreach.

**How to actually run this:**
1. Run LinkedIn ads to the list for 14-21 days for warm-up.
2. Watch for engagement (clicks, follows, page visits) — that's your real signal.
3. 1:1 outreach only goes to the subset that engaged.

If the rep wants to run direct outreach to the full list, push back. It's spam and it'll burn the list.

**Anti-pattern:** "I have a 500-account lookalike list, write me an email to send to all of them." No. Run ads first. Or layer a signal. Don't outbound a cold lookalike list.

## Targeting Customer's Competition

**Window:** Ongoing. This is a strategic motion, not a moment-in-time signal.
**Lead channel:** Email, LinkedIn, content marketing.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** If your customer is winning in a market, their competitors are losing. Their competitors know it. Position yourself as part of how the winner is winning.

**Who to target:** The same function at competitor accounts that's the buyer at your customer.

**First touch:**

> Subject: How [your customer] is approaching [problem area]
>
> [Your customer] is doing something interesting in [specific area] — they're [non-confidential strategic move]. We work with their team on [your product's role in that].
>
> If [problem area] is on your mind, the playbook they're running is unusually replicable. Happy to walk through the parts I can share.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: Send a public case study or piece of content.
- Day 12: LinkedIn touch.
- Day 21: Break.

**Anti-pattern:** Never share confidential customer details. The line is: anything publicly disclosed by the customer is fair game. Anything from your account team's internal docs is not.

## Competitive Tech Install Targeting

**Window:** Long — this is structural targeting, not event-based.
**Lead channel:** ABM (ads + email + content).
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Companies running [competitor] are ICP-validated buyers — someone there decided your category is worth paying for. They're more reachable than fully cold accounts.

**How to run:** This is the slowest play in the cold playbook. Run as a sustained ABM motion, not a single-touch cold email. Best deployed as a list to feed into ads + content + intermittent 1:1.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't lead with "we noticed you use [competitor]." Same surveillance problem as the install/uninstall plays.

## Intent Signal Targeting

**Window:** 7-14 days from the spike.
**Lead channel:** Email + LinkedIn ad layering.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Third-party intent data (Bombora, G2, ZoomInfo, 6sense) flags accounts researching topics. It's directional, not deterministic — most intent flags are noise. Treat it as a filter to find a smaller pool, then layer a real signal.

**How to run:** Don't write outreach off intent data alone. Use it to prioritize which accounts to investigate further. Once you find a real person doing a real thing at one of those accounts, run the appropriate single-signal play (site visit, social activity, etc.).

**Anti-pattern:** Mass email blasts to "high-intent" accounts. The intent score is too noisy to justify a personalized opener.

## Social Activity Targeting

**Window:** 24-72 hours from the activity.
**Lead channel:** LinkedIn message, then email.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Someone publicly posting/commenting/engaging with your category content is signaling interest. The opener writes itself: reference the actual post.

**First touch (LinkedIn DM):**

> Saw your post about [specific topic]. The point about [specific thing they said] resonated — most [function] leaders I talk to are dealing with the same thing. We've helped [comparable company] with [specific outcome]. No pitch — just curious if you're seeing it the same way.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't comment-then-DM in rapid succession. It looks like a tactic. Pick one.

## Fiscal Year End Targeting

**Window:** 60-90 days before fiscal year end. The "use it or lose it" budget window.
**Lead channel:** Email, then call.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Companies with fiscal-year-end budget that hasn't been spent will spend it. Q4 in calendar-year companies, but worth checking — many enterprises run on different fiscal calendars (Microsoft is July; Apple is September; many federal contractors are September).

**First touch:**

> Subject: Q4 budget question
>
> Most [function] teams I work with hit the same situation in the last quarter: budget allocated, not yet deployed, and a real desire not to give it back. If that's the situation at [company], we can typically scope and start a project in 2-3 weeks. Worth 15 minutes?

**Anti-pattern:** Don't run this play in October to companies on July fiscal years. Check the calendar before sending.

## Event Targeting

**Window:** 14 days before through 14 days after the event.
**Lead channel:** Email pre-event, in-person at, email post-event.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Events concentrate buyers in a specific topic in a specific location. Pre-event reach has the highest open rates of the year for an event-attendee list.

**Pre-event first touch:**

> Subject: [Event] next week
>
> Going to [event] next week? We're [doing X / hosting Y / sponsoring Z]. If you've got 20 minutes between sessions, I'd like to introduce you to [specific value]. Coffee or beer, your call.

**Post-event first touch:**

> Subject: After [event]
>
> [Event] takeaway that's stuck with me: [one specific insight, your own]. Curious what landed for you.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't send "schedule a meeting at the booth" emails. They get deleted. Lead with what you'll talk about, not where.

## OOO Welcome Back

**Window:** 1-3 days after their out-of-office reply ends.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Inbox-zero moment. Senior people often clear inbox aggressively in the first 48 hours back, but they read the things that look genuinely useful.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Welcome back — quick one
>
> Hope the time off was good. Wanted to make sure this didn't get lost in the inbox-zero pile. [One sentence of context + ask.]

**Anti-pattern:** Don't pretend you didn't know they were out. Reference it briefly and move on.

## Physical Mail

**Window:** Send mid-week, follow up with email 2-3 days after expected arrival.
**Lead channel:** Mail, then email.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Physical mail to enterprise execs cuts through inbox saturation. Works for high-ACV deals only — the unit economics fall apart below ~$50K ACV.

**How to run:** A physical artifact (book, custom report, branded item) plus a handwritten note. Then an email 2-3 days later: "did the [item] arrive? Wanted to make sure it didn't get caught in mailroom."

**Anti-pattern:** Don't send swag. Send something they'll actually use or read. A book on their problem, a custom report on their industry, a handwritten note. Swag goes in the trash.

## Account-Based Marketing

**Window:** 90+ days. ABM is a sustained motion, not a moment.
**Lead channel:** Coordinated multi-channel.
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** For named strategic accounts, no single play is enough. You need coordinated touches across channels and personas, sustained over months.

**How to run:** This is too complex for a single skill response. The components are: (1) account research, (2) ad warm-up, (3) content tailored to the account, (4) executive sponsor outreach, (5) coordinated SDR multi-thread, (6) field/event presence. If the user is asking for ABM as a single play, push back: ask which specific account they're trying to break into and run a per-account signal play instead.

**Anti-pattern:** Treating ABM as "write a personalized email." That's just personalized cold email. ABM is a multi-quarter, multi-channel motion.

---

## Custom Messages in Chat

**Window:** Real-time.
**Lead channel:** On-site chat.
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** A targeted chat trigger to a known account on a high-intent page converts at 5-10x of cold email. The hard part is the targeting and trigger logic — usually owned by demand gen, not sales.

**How to run:** This is a marketing-ops play, not a rep play. If the user is a rep asking for this, they're in the wrong place — point them to demand gen.

---

## Product-Specific Targeting

**Window:** Variable, depending on product launch cycle.
**Lead channel:** Email, ads.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Reps targeting based on a specific product or use case (rather than broad ICP) get higher response rates because the message is sharper.

**How to run:** Build the persona around the *use case*, not the title. "VP of Eng who runs a Kubernetes-heavy infra at 200+ engineers" is a better target than "VP of Engineering, 500+ headcount."

**Anti-pattern:** Don't list 5 products in one email. Pick one. Send a different email for each product.
