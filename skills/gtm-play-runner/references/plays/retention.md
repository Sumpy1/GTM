# Retention — 8 Plays

Retention is the highest-ROI work in GTM. A 5-point improvement in retention is worth more than the same effort in new sales — both because keeping a customer is cheaper than acquiring one and because retained customers expand. The mistake CSMs make is treating retention as a renewal-cycle event. It's not. It's a 365-day motion that culminates in renewal.

The hierarchy:

1. **Onboarding sales flows** — most churn is decided in the first 90 days.
2. **Low adoption engagement** — catch usage drops early.
3. **At-risk customer surveys** — formalize the diagnostic before renewal.
4. **Upcoming renewals** — the renewal motion itself.
5. **Congratulate promotions** — keep the relationship warm with the buyer's career.
6. **Customer awards / recognition** — celebrate publicly, deepen privately.
7. **General user engagement** — community, advocacy, lateral relationships.
8. **Automate customer referrals** — turn happy customers into pipeline.

---

## Onboarding Sales Flows

**Window:** First 30, 60, 90 days. After that, you've already lost or won.
**Lead channel:** In-product + scheduled CS touches + email.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** Most churn is determined in the first 90 days. A customer who hasn't seen value by day 60 is statistically much more likely to churn at month 11. The play is structured: specific milestones, specific touches, specific interventions if they stall.

**The 30/60/90 framework:**

- **Day 30:** First value milestone hit. Customer can articulate one specific outcome.
- **Day 60:** Second persona engaged. The relationship is no longer single-threaded.
- **Day 90:** Executive review or formal QBR. The exec sponsor on the customer side has seen the value tied to their priorities.

**Run the play by:**
1. Day 1: Welcome from CS lead + clear "what success looks like" doc.
2. Day 14: Check on first milestone progress. Intervene if behind.
3. Day 30: Confirm milestone hit. If not, escalate.
4. Day 60: Identify second persona. Run a Multi-threading Opportunities-style play on the customer.
5. Day 90: Executive review.

**Anti-pattern:** Light-touch onboarding for SMB customers. The unit economics still favor a real onboarding because the alternative is high churn. Match touch density to deal size, but never zero.

## Low Adoption Engagement

**Window:** As soon as usage drops below a threshold.
**Lead channel:** Email + call + in-product.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Low adoption is the leading indicator of churn. By the time the customer starts negotiating renewal, low adoption is locked in. The play is to catch the usage drop early and intervene with help — training, new use cases, persona expansion.

**How to run:** Need a usage signal — daily active users, key feature usage, time-since-login. Threshold should be specific to the product but typically: 30% drop month-over-month or 14 days no login for active users.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Saw a usage shift — anything I can help with?
>
> Noticed [specific usage drop — be honest about what you saw]. Sometimes that's a sign of a team change or a process shift; sometimes it's a sign that something isn't quite working. Want to grab 20 minutes to figure out which one and what would help?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: Call if no email response.
- Day 10: Loop in the executive sponsor on the customer side.
- Day 14: Escalate to your CS leadership.

**Anti-pattern:** Sending automated "we miss you" emails. Customers can smell the auto-pilot. Make the touch specific to their actual data.

## At-Risk Customer Surveys

**Window:** 90-120 days before renewal. Earlier than that is too speculative; later is too late.
**Lead channel:** Email survey + follow-up call.
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** A formal "are we doing well?" survey before renewal forces the conversation that should be happening informally. The customer either tells you they're happy (gold for renewal) or surfaces issues you have time to fix.

**The survey:**

The temptation is to make this a 12-question NPS-style survey. Don't. Three questions:

1. **What's the most valuable thing we've delivered in the last year?** (If they can't answer, you have a problem.)
2. **What would make us indispensable that we're not doing today?** (Surfaces upsell + product gaps.)
3. **If you were starting over today, would you choose us again?** (The honest churn signal.)

**First touch:**

> Subject: Three questions before renewal
>
> We have your renewal coming up in [N] days. Before we get into the contract conversation, want to make sure we're delivering what you actually need. Three short questions:
>
> 1. What's the most valuable thing we've delivered this year?
> 2. What would make us indispensable that we're not doing today?
> 3. If you were starting over today, would you choose us again?
>
> Honest answers help me — and the team — show up the right way for the renewal conversation.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: Call to walk through the answers.
- Day 14: Translate answers into a "renewal plan" doc and share back.

**Anti-pattern:** Sending the survey and then not addressing the answers. If the customer says "you're not delivering on X," and the next thing they get is a renewal quote, they will churn. Survey results have to drive action.

## Upcoming Renewals

**Window:** 90 days, 60 days, 30 days, and 14 days out.
**Lead channel:** Email + call + executive touch.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Renewal motions that start at 30 days lose. The customer hasn't been thinking about you, has no urgency, and procurement adds 30+ days on its own. The play starts at 90.

**The cadence:**

- **Day -90:** Run the at-risk survey (or its equivalent informal conversation).
- **Day -60:** Renewal terms conversation. Surface any pricing, scope, or seat changes.
- **Day -45:** Send draft renewal paperwork.
- **Day -30:** Active follow-up on signature.
- **Day -14:** Escalate if not signed.

**First touch (Day -90):**

> Subject: Renewal — and what comes next
>
> Your renewal is coming up on [date]. Before we get into the contract, want to use the next 90 days to (a) make sure we're delivering on what matters and (b) talk through what changes for next year — new use cases, new teams, new outcomes.
>
> Want to grab an hour in the next two weeks to do that?

**Anti-pattern:** Sending the renewal paperwork as the first touch in the renewal cycle. It signals you're treating it as a transaction, not a relationship.

## Congratulate Promotions

**Window:** Within 7 days of the promotion.
**Lead channel:** LinkedIn message, then email.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** A customer contact getting promoted is a positive event for the relationship — they have more authority, they may have new budget, they're more likely to be in the room for renewal. Reaching out warmly cements the relationship at the higher level.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Congrats on the move
>
> Saw the promotion to [new title]. Well-deserved. Excited to see what you do in the new role — and happy to talk through how our work together fits into the new mandate when the dust settles.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 30: Light check-in — "how's the new role going?"
- Day 60: Strategic conversation about expanded scope.

**Anti-pattern:** Immediate upsell pitch after the congrats. Wait. Let them get settled. The play is to be the warm voice in their inbox, not another vendor asking for things.

## Customer Awards or Recognition

**Window:** Within 7 days of the award.
**Lead channel:** LinkedIn + email + amplification.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Public recognition is a relationship-deepening moment. The play is to amplify the customer's win publicly (LinkedIn share, internal Slack mention) and acknowledge it privately. The customer remembers who showed up.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Congrats on [award]
>
> Saw [company] won [award]. Well-earned — the work [specific person] and team have done in [area] is the reason. Sharing on our side too. Anything we can do to amplify, let me know.

**Anti-pattern:** Tying the congrats to a sales ask. Just congratulate. The relationship deepening is the goal.

## General User Engagement

**Window:** Ongoing.
**Lead channel:** Community, events, content.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Customers who are engaged with your community — user groups, customer advisory boards, public events — churn at meaningfully lower rates. The play is structural: invite the customer's team to your community programs, not just the buyer.

**How to run:** This is a CS-marketing collaboration, not a single email. Build a list of "engaged users" beyond the buyer. Invite them to user group events, beta programs, customer advisory boards. Track participation as a renewal leading indicator.

**Anti-pattern:** Treating the buyer as the only relationship. End users determine adoption; adoption determines renewal.

## Automate Customer Referrals

**Window:** When NPS is high or after a successful milestone.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Happy customers are willing to refer, but rarely volunteer. A specific, well-timed ask converts at meaningful rates.

**The timing window:** Right after a successful business outcome — not at random intervals. "You just hit [milestone]" is the trigger.

**First touch:**

> Subject: After [milestone] — quick ask
>
> Now that [milestone] is in the books, want to ask something I rarely ask: is there one peer at another company who'd benefit from a conversation with us? No pressure if not — and if there is, a one-line intro is all I'd need.

**Anti-pattern:** Routine "refer a friend" emails. They get ignored. The play depends on the trigger being a real, specific success.
