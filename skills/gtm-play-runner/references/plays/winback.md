# Winback — 2 Plays

The two plays here are very different. "Win back churned customers" is recovering a lost account; "Contact external moves" is following your champion to their next company. Both are high-leverage, both are time-sensitive, and both fail when run too aggressively.

---

## Win Back Churned Customers

**Window:** 6-12 months after churn. Earlier is too raw; later is too late.
**Lead channel:** Email, then call if they engage.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Churned customers know your product. They evaluated you, bought you, used you, and decided to leave. Often the reason they left has changed — your product has improved, the alternative they chose disappointed them, the budget situation changed, or a leadership change shifted priorities. The play is to find the change and use it as the new opener.

**The 6-12 month timing:** Earlier and the wound is fresh — they're defensive about the decision to leave. Later and they've fully migrated to the alternative and don't want to switch back. The 6-12 month window is when the alternative has had time to disappoint and the original choice rationale has had time to fade.

**How to run:**

Step 1: Identify the reason for churn. Was it price, fit, product, relationship, or budget? Pull from CRM notes, exit conversations, win/loss interviews.

Step 2: Identify what's changed. Has your product changed? Has their company changed (new leadership, new strategy)? Has their alternative had public stumbles?

Step 3: Identify the right contact. The original buyer may have left. The original detractor may have left. New buyer = new opportunity.

Step 4: Open with the change, not the relationship.

**First touch (product changed):**

> Subject: We fixed the thing that pushed you out
>
> [Time] back, [company] left us — and you were clear that [specific reason]. Reaching out because we just shipped [specific change directly addressing the reason]. Not pitching a return; just thought you'd want to know.
>
> If it's worth re-opening the conversation, I'm here. If not, no pressure — just wanted to close the loop.

**First touch (their situation changed):**

> Subject: Heard about [change at their company]
>
> Saw [news / leadership change / market shift] at [company]. Brings to mind our last conversation, when [original reason for churn] was the blocker. The landscape's pretty different now — worth a check-in to see if it's worth re-evaluating?

**First touch (the alternative is failing them):**

> Subject: Saw the news on [their current vendor]
>
> [Specific news about the vendor they chose — outage, acquisition, layoffs, product issue]. Bringing back the conversation we had [time] ago, since this might be the right moment to look again. No pitch — just letting you know we're here.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 7: Light follow-up.
- Day 14: One more touch with a specific resource.
- Day 21: Break — set a 6-month reminder.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 touches. Move to a long-tail nurture.

**Anti-pattern:** Re-engaging without naming the original reason for churn. They remember why they left. Pretending it didn't happen makes you look uninformed. Naming it makes you look credible.

## Contact External Moves

**Window:** First 30-60 days of the new role.
**Lead channel:** Email or LinkedIn DM, warm and brief.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Following a former champion to their new company is the single highest-converting cold-ish signal in B2B sales. They already know your product. They already trust you. They're choosing their stack at the new place. The cost of acquisition is near-zero — the only question is whether you show up at the right time.

**Why this is in the winback file:** Some companies categorize this as cold (it's a new account). Most senior operators put it here because the relationship is the asset, not the company. You're winning back the *person*.

**The 30-60 day window:** Earlier than 30 days they're drinking from a firehose. Later than 60 they've already started making tooling decisions. Day 45 is the sweet spot.

**How to run:**

Step 1: Don't pitch in the first email. The first email is a "congratulations, I'm here when you're ready" note. Period.

Step 2: Watch for engagement. If they reply warmly, that's permission to bring up business at the next touch. If they don't, give them space.

Step 3: When the conversation does start, treat the new company as a new evaluation. Don't assume your old terms apply, don't assume the same use case fits, don't assume their team will have the same tolerance for your product's quirks.

**First touch:**

> Subject: New chapter
>
> Saw the move to [new company] — congrats, it's a great fit for the work you did at [old company].
>
> No pitch here. Just wanted to say: when you're ready to think about the [tool category] stack at [new company], you know where to find us. Happy to bring my team in for a working session whenever it's useful — or to stay out of the way if you're handling it internally.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 7-14: LinkedIn message, friendly, no business.
- Day 30-45: Light check-in. "Settling in?"
- Day 60: If still nothing, one resource share + an open-ended question about their priorities.
- Day 90: Break — set a quarterly reminder.

**Stop condition:** No response after 3 friendly touches over 90 days. They may not own the decision in the new role, or the timing's wrong. Stay warm, stop initiating.

**Anti-pattern (the most common one):** Pitching pricing or "previous customer" terms in the first email. It cheapens the relationship. They moved to make their mark at a new company, not to recreate their last setup. Let them ask about pricing; don't lead with it.

**Second anti-pattern:** Assuming the previous champion is your champion at the new company. They might be on a 90-day plan to clean house — including ripping out anything that reminds them of the old place. Read the room. Some moves are continuity moves; some are clean-break moves. Treat differently.
