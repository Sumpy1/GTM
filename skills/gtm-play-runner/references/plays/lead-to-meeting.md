# Lead → Meeting — 8 Plays

These are the highest-yield plays in the entire library, because the buyer has already raised their hand. The mistake reps make here is treating these leads like cold outreach. They're not. The job is to remove friction, not to convince.

The hierarchy:

1. **Form fills** — fastest possible response wins. Speed beats craft.
2. **Trial signups** — same as above, plus product-led messaging.
3. **Content downloads** — interest is real but soft. Nurture, don't sell.
4. **Abandoned forms / chats** — friction caused them to drop. Remove the friction.
5. **Recycled leads** — old leads, now warmer. Treat as semi-cold.

---

## Fast SLA on Form Fill

**Window:** 5 minutes. Response rates drop ~80% after the first hour and ~99% after a day.
**Lead channel:** Phone (yes, really), then email.
**Difficulty:** Expert (because it requires operational discipline). **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** A form fill is a hand raise. The lead is at peak interest in the moments after they fill out the form — they're probably still on your site. Every minute that passes, conversion to meeting drops measurably. Harvard Business Review's old study put the difference between 5-minute and 30-minute response at roughly 21x. The number's argued, but the direction isn't.

**How to run:** This is an *operational* play, not a creative one. The work is making sure the rep is actually available to call within 5 minutes during business hours. Routing rules, mobile alerts, slack pings — that's the play.

**First touch (call):**

Cold open: "Hi [name], this is [your name] from [company]. You just filled out a form on our site about [specific topic] — I happened to see it come in and wanted to catch you while it was top of mind. Got 2 minutes for one question?"

The "got 2 minutes" framing is critical. You're not asking for a meeting yet. You're asking for permission to keep talking.

**If no answer:** Email immediately, not later.

> Subject: Just tried calling
>
> Tried you on [number]. Saw your form come through about [topic] — wanted to catch you fast in case it's time-sensitive. Easiest path: [Calendly link] or just reply with a window that works.

**Follow-up sequence (only if no response to the first call+email):**
- Hour 4: Second call attempt.
- Day 2: LinkedIn connect with one-line.
- Day 4: Final email — "wrapping this up unless I hear back."
- Day 7: Recycle.

**Stop condition:** No response after 4 touches in 7 days. Move to recycle queue.

**Anti-pattern:** Treating form fills like cold outreach with carefully crafted day-3 follow-ups. By day 3, the lead is gone. Speed beats craft on this one specifically.

## Trial Provisioning Flow

**Window:** First 24-72 hours of trial. Best at the moment they actually log in for the second time.
**Lead channel:** In-product + email + call (in that order).
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** A trial signup is the strongest possible signal short of a sales conversation. They're already evaluating. The play is to remove friction from getting to value, not to schedule a sales call before they've experienced the product.

**How to run:** The first email should not ask for a meeting. It should help them succeed in the product.

**First touch:**

> Subject: 5 minutes to your first [outcome]
>
> Welcome to [product]. The fastest path to seeing what it does:
>
> 1. [Specific first step]
> 2. [Specific second step]
> 3. [Specific third step]
>
> If you hit a wall on any of those, hit reply — I read these directly.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 2: If they've activated, one-line check-in: "saw you got to [milestone] — what's next on your list?"
- Day 4: If they've stalled, offer help. "Saw the trial is paused — anything I can unblock?"
- Day 7: First sales-flavored ask: "Worth 20 minutes to talk through your use case?"
- Day 14: Trial expiration nudge.

**Stop condition:** If they don't activate by day 7, they probably won't. Move to a long-tail nurture.

**Anti-pattern:** Pitching pricing on day 1. They haven't seen the value yet. The product needs to do its job before sales does.

## Auto Nurture Content Download

**Window:** 14 days for the warm window, 90 days for nurture.
**Lead channel:** Email primarily, light LinkedIn.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** A content download is a soft signal. They're interested in the topic, not necessarily ready to buy. The play is to deepen the topical relationship, not to sell.

**How to run:** Don't ask for a meeting in the first email. Send adjacent content. Let the lead self-qualify by engaging with more.

**First touch:**

> Subject: After [content title]
>
> Saw you downloaded [content title]. The piece most readers tell me they wish I'd added: [specific tactical thing related to the topic].
>
> Two follow-ups in the same vein, in case useful:
> - [Resource 1]
> - [Resource 2]
>
> If you ever want to talk through how this applies to [their company / industry], I'm around.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 7: Send one more adjacent resource, no ask.
- Day 14: Soft ask. "Useful at all? Happy to do a 20-min walkthrough if helpful."
- Day 30: Move to nurture cadence.

**Stop condition:** No engagement (open, click) after 3 emails. Move to long-tail.

**Anti-pattern:** Treating a content downloader like a hot lead. They're a researcher, not a buyer. Pitching too early kills the relationship.

## Abandoned Form Follow-Up

**Window:** 24-48 hours from abandonment.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** They started filling out a form, hit something (length, a required field, a thought), and bounced. Reaching out with a low-friction alternative recovers a real chunk of these.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Saw you stopped halfway
>
> No pressure — just noticed you started filling out the [form name] and dropped off. If the form was the friction, you can also just reply to this email with [the one piece of info we actually need] and I'll take it from there.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 4: One more email with the simplified ask.
- Day 7: Break.

**Stop condition:** Two touches, no response. They weren't serious or chose another path.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't sound surveillance-y. "I noticed you stopped at field 3" is creepy. "I saw you started and didn't finish" is fine.

## Abandoned Form — Calendar Booking

**Window:** Same as above — 24-48 hours.
**Lead channel:** Email with calendar link.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Variant of abandoned form for cases where the natural next step is a meeting, not a sales process. Removes form friction by going straight to a calendar.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Easier path
>
> Saw you started the form. If a 20-minute call is faster, here's my calendar: [link]. Or reply with two windows that work and I'll send the invite.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't send both this play and the standard abandoned form play. Pick one. The user's decision is whether the natural next step is async or a meeting.

## Abandoned Chat Follow-Up

**Window:** 4-24 hours.
**Lead channel:** Email + chat re-engagement if they return to site.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Chat abandonment is hotter than form abandonment because the user typed something — there's a question they didn't get answered. Send the answer.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Following up on your chat
>
> Saw you came in via chat earlier and asked about [their actual question]. Short answer: [actual answer]. Longer version + how it applies to [their company]: happy to walk through. Want me to send notes or take 15 minutes?

**Anti-pattern:** Sending a generic "sorry we missed you" without addressing the specific question they typed. The whole reason this play has high impact is the specificity.

## Recycle Leads

**Window:** 90+ days from last touch on cold leads. 30+ days for content downloaders who didn't engage further.
**Lead channel:** Email, fresh angle.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Old leads get re-warmed when something changes — at their company, in your product, or in the market. The play is to find the change and use it as the new opener.

**How to run:** Never recycle a lead with the same message that didn't work the first time. Find a new hook: a recent product launch, a new case study, news at their company, a role change. If you can't find a new hook, don't recycle yet.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Picking this back up
>
> We talked / you reached out about [topic] [time ago]. Reaching back out because [specific new development — your product launch, their company news, market shift].
>
> Worth re-opening the conversation? If it's not the right time, just say "not now" and I'll set a reminder.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: One follow-up, lighter.
- Day 10: Break — set a 90-day reminder.

**Stop condition:** No response after 2 touches. Set a long-tail nurture.

**Anti-pattern:** Recycling a lead 17 times over 4 years. After the second or third recycle with no engagement, accept that they're not coming back. Move them to a marketing list and stop running 1:1 motion.
