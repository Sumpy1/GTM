# Opportunity → Close — 8 Plays

These are the plays for active deals. The mistake reps make in this stage is doing too much of the wrong thing — too many "checking in" emails, not enough actual movement on the deal. The job here is to move the deal forward or kill it. Wishing it forward is the third option, and it's the worst one.

The hierarchy:

1. **Qualifying out** — the most underrated play in B2B sales. Killing a bad deal frees up time for good ones.
2. **Multi-threading** — the single highest-ROI thing a rep can do on a real deal.
3. **Replace champion** — when the wheels come off and the original buyer leaves.
4. **Stalled opportunity recovery** — the deal has gone quiet; do something specific.
5. **e-Sign follow-up** — contract is out; nudge without nagging.
6. **Competitor mentions** — they brought up a competitor; address it directly.
7. **Persona expansion** — adding new buyer types to an existing deal.
8. **Lost deal re-engage** — the deal closed-lost; bring it back at the right moment.

## Qualifying Out Opportunities

**Window:** Anytime. The earlier the better.
**Lead channel:** Email or call.
**Difficulty:** Easy operationally, hard psychologically. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** Most reps' pipelines are clogged with deals that won't close. Forecast accuracy improves dramatically when reps will actually call a deal dead instead of leaving it at "30% probability" for two more quarters. This play is permission to do that.

**The senior operator's framing:** A deal you kill in Q1 is a deal you're not pretending to forecast in Q3. Your manager will respect you more for accurate forecasting than for a fat-but-fake pipeline.

**The diagnostic before running:**

A deal should be qualified out if any of these are true:
- No identified economic buyer after [N] meetings
- No agreed-upon next step on the calendar
- 30+ days since meaningful response
- Buyer has changed roles or left
- Customer told you a competitor is "ahead"
- Procurement has gone quiet for 45+ days

**First touch (the "courage email"):**

> Subject: Honest read
>
> Want to give you an honest read on where I think we are. From what I'm seeing on my side — [specific signals: gaps in next steps, lack of buyer engagement, etc.] — this doesn't feel like a deal that's going to close in [timeframe]. I'd rather hear that I'm wrong than keep pushing on something that isn't going to land.
>
> Two questions:
> 1. Am I reading this right, or am I missing something?
> 2. If I'm right, what's the most useful path — pause until [trigger], close-lost, or something else?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: One follow-up if no response.
- Day 10: Mark closed-lost. Set a 90-day reminder for re-engage.

**Anti-pattern:** Avoiding this play because killing a deal feels bad. It feels worse to forecast it for six months and miss your number.

## Multi-threading Opportunities

**Window:** Once you have one engaged contact, immediately. The longer you wait, the harder it gets.
**Lead channel:** LinkedIn for warm intros, then email.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Single-threaded deals lose at ~70% rate when the champion leaves, gets reorged, or simply goes on vacation. Multi-threaded deals lose at much lower rates because you have redundancy. The single biggest predictor of close rate in enterprise sales is "number of contacts engaged at the account."

**How to run:** With your existing champion's blessing if possible, without it if necessary. The right way is to ask: "Who else on your team should be part of this conversation? I don't want to come at you sideways."

**First touch (with champion's blessing):**

> Subject: Other folks I should meet
>
> As we move forward, who on your team — finance, security, end users — should I get in front of? I'd rather make those introductions through you now than have surprise stakeholders show up at the contract stage.

**First touch (without champion's blessing — to a peer of the champion):**

> Subject: Working with [champion] on [project]
>
> [Champion's name] and I have been working through [project] — wanted to introduce myself directly because the work touches your team's [specific responsibility]. Happy to share what we're discussing or just stay on your radar.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 3: Light LinkedIn touch.
- Day 7: One more email.
- Day 14: Loop back to champion.

**Anti-pattern:** Going around the champion without telling them. If the champion finds out (and they will), the deal is dead. Always loop them in.

## Replace Deal Champion

**Window:** Within 14 days of the champion leaving. The longer the gap, the colder the new contact.
**Lead channel:** Email + LinkedIn + warm intro from departed champion if possible.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Deals where the champion leaves close at <15% without active replacement. With active replacement, close rates can be saved to 40-50%. The play is: don't wait, don't assume the next person will pick it up, and use the original champion to make warm intros while they still care.

**How to run:** Step 1 is reaching out to the *departing* champion before they leave. Step 2 is identifying their replacement. Step 3 is a warm intro from the departing champion. If you can't get the warm intro, the play is harder but still runnable.

**First touch (to departing champion):**

> Subject: Before you go
>
> Heard the news — congrats on the move. Before you transition, two asks:
> 1. Who's picking up [project] on your team? I want to make sure it doesn't drop.
> 2. Would you be willing to make a warm intro? Even a one-line "this person is good to work with" goes a long way.

**First touch (to replacement, with warm intro):**

> Subject: Picking up [project]
>
> [Departed champion] mentioned you'd be the right person to continue the conversation we were having about [project]. Quick context on where things stand: [3 sentences max]. Want to set up a 30-minute working session to make sure my read on priorities matches yours?

**First touch (to replacement, without warm intro):**

> Subject: [Project] — quick handoff context
>
> [Departed champion]'s departure leaves [project] without a clear owner on your side. We've been working on [3-sentence summary]. Worth 30 minutes to talk through whether and how you want to continue? Or if it's a different priority for you, I'd rather know now.

**Anti-pattern:** Treating the new buyer like the old one. They have their own priorities, possibly different from the previous champion's. Re-discover; don't assume.

## Stalled Opportunity

**Window:** Run when the deal has been silent for 14-21 days, not before.
**Lead channel:** Email, then call, then break.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Stalled deals have one of three causes: (a) priorities shifted, (b) a stakeholder is blocking, (c) the deal is actually dead and the buyer hasn't told you yet. The play is to surface which one and act on it.

**The senior operator's read:** Most "stalled" deals are dead. The buyer is being polite. The faster you can get them to admit it, the faster you can either revive it or move on.

**First touch (the "is this real" email):**

> Subject: Quick gut-check
>
> It's been [N] days since we last connected on [project]. A few honest possibilities I want to check:
>
> 1. Priorities shifted and this isn't current.
> 2. Internal blocker we haven't talked through.
> 3. Still on track, just busy.
>
> Reply with the number that fits — even (1) is useful. I'd rather know the real answer than keep guessing.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: Call.
- Day 10: Final email — "wrapping this up unless I hear back."
- Day 14: Move to closed-lost or pause.

**Anti-pattern:** Sending "checking in" emails for 6 weeks. They're filler. The numbered framing forces a decision; "checking in" doesn't.

## e-Sign Follow Up

**Window:** 48 hours after sending, then every 3-5 days.
**Lead channel:** Email primarily.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** Contracts in DocuSign / similar tools have a high rate of going stale because nobody re-prompts. The buyer signed off on terms but signing the actual paper got bumped by 10 other priorities. A specific, light nudge moves it.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Contract — anything to clarify?
>
> The contract has been sitting in your inbox for 48 hours. Usually that means one of two things:
>
> 1. Legal/procurement is reviewing and it's just timing.
> 2. Something in the redlines isn't quite right and a quick conversation would unblock it.
>
> Which is it? Either way, easy to handle — just want to make sure it's not stuck on something solvable.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 4: Light touch — "checking on (1) vs (2) from earlier."
- Day 7: Loop in champion or procurement directly.
- Day 14: Escalate or kill.

**Anti-pattern:** Waiting more than 48 hours for the first nudge. The longer the contract sits, the more likely something organizational has shifted.

## Competitor Mentions

**Window:** Real-time, in the conversation. Then 24-48 hours after.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Expert. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** When a buyer brings up a competitor, the worst response is to disparage them. The best response is to acknowledge what the competitor does well, then sharpen the specific dimension where you win. The follow-up email puts that comparison in writing.

**How to run:** After the meeting, send a head-to-head doc that's *honest* about both products. The buyer respects honesty more than aggression. The dimensions you choose to compare on are the play — pick the ones where you win.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Side-by-side on [Competitor]
>
> You mentioned [Competitor] in our conversation — wanted to put together an honest comparison rather than leave it abstract. Where they're stronger: [one specific area]. Where we're stronger: [two specific areas]. The decision usually comes down to [the dimension you actually win on].
>
> Happy to do a working session on the comparison if it'd help.

**Anti-pattern:** Trash-talking the competitor. The buyer probably knows them better than you do. They'll catch the dishonesty.

## Persona Expansion

**Window:** Mid-deal, when you're hitting a ceiling with the current persona.
**Lead channel:** Multi-channel.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Sometimes the deal stalls because the buyer in the room can't actually authorize it. Persona expansion is bringing in the right titles — finance for budget, IT for security, end users for adoption proof — to unblock.

**How to run:** Diagnose what's blocking. Then identify which persona's involvement removes the block. Then go get them, with the existing buyer's blessing.

**Anti-pattern:** Adding personas to look like progress. If the deal isn't stalled on something a new persona solves, this play just adds noise.

## Lost Deal Re-Engage

**Window:** 90-180 days after closed-lost. Sooner is too soon (still raw); later means the situation may have settled into the wrong tooling permanently.
**Lead channel:** Email.
**Difficulty:** Easy. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** Closed-lost deals have a higher conversion rate than cold accounts because the buyer already evaluated you. The play depends on having a real reason to come back: a product change, a market shift, a problem with their chosen vendor.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Picking this back up — [reason]
>
> When we last talked, you went with [competitor / status quo]. Reaching back out because [specific new development on your side, or specific industry/vendor news that's relevant to them].
>
> Worth re-opening the conversation, or is the current setup working?

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 5: One follow-up.
- Day 10: Break — re-set the 90-day reminder.

**Anti-pattern:** Re-engaging without a new hook. "Just checking in 6 months later" is the worst version of this play. Find the change.
