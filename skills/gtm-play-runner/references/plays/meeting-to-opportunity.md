# Meeting → Opportunity — 6 Plays

The gap between "meeting booked" and "opportunity created" is where most pipeline dies. The mistake reps make is treating the meeting as the win. The meeting is the start. These plays exist to make sure (a) the meeting actually happens, (b) it's productive when it does, and (c) something happens after.

The hierarchy:

1. **Pre-meeting confirmation** — protect the meeting from no-shows.
2. **Pre-meeting survey** — turn a discovery call into a working session.
3. **Pre-meeting aircover** — ABM-style warm-up for high-stakes meetings.
4. **Meeting no-show recovery** — the meeting didn't happen; salvage it.
5. **Completed meeting, no opp** — the meeting happened; nothing came of it. Diagnose.
6. **Podcast mention** — they appeared publicly; use it as a deepening touch.

---

## Pre-Meeting Confirmation

**Window:** 24-48 hours before meeting.
**Lead channel:** Email, with a calendar invite verification.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** No-show rates drop substantially when there's a confirmation touch the day before. Calendar invites alone aren't enough — people accept them and forget.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Quick confirm — [Day] [Time]
>
> Looking forward to talking [Day] at [Time]. Two things to make the time useful:
>
> 1. [One specific question you'd like them to think about]
> 2. [One thing you'll bring — a doc, a comparison, a customer reference]
>
> If anything's changed, easy to move — just reply.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- 2 hours before: Optional second touch only for high-stakes meetings — "see you in 2 hours."

**Anti-pattern:** A "looking forward" email with no content. The point of this email is to seed the meeting agenda, not to remind them. The reminder is the calendar invite's job.

## Pre-Meeting Survey

**Window:** 3-5 days before meeting. Long enough that they have time to fill it out, short enough that the meeting is real to them.
**Lead channel:** Email with a 3-question survey.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** A 3-question pre-meeting survey turns first meetings from generic discovery into targeted conversations. Senior buyers respect a rep who shows up prepared. Junior buyers are flattered to be asked. Fill rate is ~30-50% even on cold-ish meetings — and even unanswered questions become the agenda.

**The three questions:**

1. **What problem brought you to this meeting?** (Not "what's your priority" — too broad.)
2. **Who else needs to be in the room when we're solving this?** (Surfaces decision-makers you don't know about.)
3. **What would make this meeting feel like a waste of time?** (Get the failure mode out in the open.)

These three are battle-tested. Don't add more. Don't change them unless you've replaced them with something at least as sharp.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Three questions before we meet [Day]
>
> Want to make our meeting on [Day] as useful as possible. If you have 3 minutes, three quick questions — answers help me come prepared with the right material:
>
> 1. What problem brought you to this conversation?
> 2. Who else should be in the room when we work on this?
> 3. What would make this meeting feel like a waste of time?
>
> If three minutes is too much, the second one is the most useful.

**Anti-pattern:** Sending a 12-question survey. Nobody fills those out. Three is the maximum.

## Pre-Meeting Aircover

**Window:** 3-7 days before meeting.
**Lead channel:** Coordinated multi-channel — exec email + LinkedIn touches from team + content delivery.
**Difficulty:** Easy operationally, but requires a team. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** For high-stakes meetings, having multiple touches from your side in the days leading up keeps your team top-of-mind and signals that you're treating the meeting as important. Common mistake: only the rep on the meeting communicates.

**How to run:**
- Day -7: Rep sends prep email (or pre-meeting survey).
- Day -5: An exec from your side sends a one-line LinkedIn note: "looking forward to my team's conversation with you on Friday."
- Day -3: Send a relevant case study or one-pager.
- Day -1: Confirmation email.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't have 5 different people send 5 different emails. The exec note should be one line, the case study should be the rep's email. Coordinate the touches; don't carpet-bomb.

## Meeting No-Shows

**Window:** Within 1 hour of the missed meeting, then again at 24 hours.
**Lead channel:** Phone (if you have the number), then email.
**Difficulty:** Expert (because tone matters). **Impact:** High.

**Why it works:** Most no-shows are not malicious — they're calendar accidents, fire drills, or a junior person who didn't know they were the decision-maker. A good no-show recovery converts ~30% of these to rebooked meetings. The bad version converts <5% and burns the relationship.

**First touch (within 1 hour):**

> Subject: Missed you — happens
>
> Didn't see you on the [Time] call — assuming something came up. Easiest path to reschedule: [Calendar link]. Or just reply with two windows.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 1: If no response, second touch — slightly warmer, with a hook. "Wanted to make sure this didn't get lost. The thing I was going to walk through: [specific value]."
- Day 4: Final touch — "wrapping up unless I hear back."
- Day 7: Break.

**Stop condition:** Three touches, no response. They opted out. Don't keep pushing.

**Anti-pattern:** Passive-aggressive language. "Sorry I wasn't important enough for you to show up" — versions of this leak through and they always fail. Tone is everything on this play. Be neutral, be brief, make rebooking easy.

## Completed Meeting, No Opportunity

**Window:** 24-72 hours after the meeting.
**Lead channel:** Email. **Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Medium.

**Why it works:** A meeting that didn't open an opportunity is diagnostic — you have data now you didn't have before. The mistake is to either (a) ghost them or (b) send a generic "thanks for your time, here's a follow-up." The play is to surface the actual reason it didn't progress and address it.

**The diagnostic questions to think through before drafting:**

1. **Was the timing wrong?** They have the problem but not now.
2. **Was the fit wrong?** They have a problem but not the one we solve.
3. **Was the buyer wrong?** Right company, wrong person in the meeting.
4. **Was the case wrong?** Right buyer, but our pitch didn't land.

The first touch should name what you think happened and ask if you got it right.

**First touch:**

> Subject: Following up on Tuesday
>
> Took some time to think about Tuesday's conversation. My read is that [your honest diagnosis — e.g., "this isn't a 'now' problem for [team]" or "the use case I led with isn't the one that's actually pressing"]. Want to check that with you — and if I got it right, I'd rather find the right next step than keep pushing the wrong one.

**Follow-up sequence:**
- Day 7: One more touch with a different angle if no response.
- Day 14: Break — set a 90-day reminder.

**Anti-pattern:** Pretending the meeting went great and asking for next steps as if it did. They know it didn't. Naming it builds trust; pretending burns it.

## Podcast Mention

**Window:** 7-14 days after the podcast airs.
**Lead channel:** Email or LinkedIn DM.
**Difficulty:** Intermediate. **Impact:** Very High.

**Why it works:** A buyer who appeared on a podcast just told the world what they think for an hour. You have an hour of free intel. Most reps don't use it. The ones who do convert at extraordinary rates because the email writes itself.

**How to run:** Listen to (or transcribe) the episode. Find one specific thing they said that connects to your product. Quote it back to them — accurately, not paraphrased.

**First touch:**

> Subject: [Episode title]
>
> Listened to your episode on [podcast]. The point about [specific thing they said, near-verbatim] — that's exactly the problem we work on with [function leaders] at [comparable company].
>
> Not pitching here, just want to say: if [specific operational implication of what they said] is something you're actively working on, I think there's a useful conversation to have. Worth 20 minutes?

**Anti-pattern:** Pretending you listened when you didn't. They will know. Either listen or don't run the play.
