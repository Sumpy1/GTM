# Anti-Patterns

These are the failure modes that turn good plays into bad outreach. A senior operator catches these instinctively. A junior rep doesn't see them until the deal dies. Read this when something feels off, when a play is producing weak output, or when the user is asking for outreach that feels wrong but you can't quite say why.

---

## Signal Anti-Patterns

### Treating intent data as a hard signal
Third-party intent feeds (Bombora, G2, ZoomInfo, 6sense) are *probabilistic*. A "high-intent" account often isn't actively shopping — they have one researcher, one downloader, one curious person. Writing a personalized email to a "spike" without a corroborating real-person signal produces low response rates and burns the account.

**The fix:** Use intent data to prioritize accounts to investigate, not to trigger outreach. Once you find a *real person* doing a *real thing* (visiting your pricing page, attending your event, posting in your category) at one of those accounts, run that signal's play.

### Stale signals
Funding rounds older than 30 days. Site visits older than 7 days. Promotions older than 90 days. Content downloads older than 14 days. The signals decay. The play doesn't work outside its window. The rep wrote the email anyway because the signal was the only one they had.

**The fix:** Always check the date. If the signal is stale, recommend a different play instead of running the wrong one.

### Single-signal confidence on weak signals
A lookalike score, a generic intent topic, a job-title match — none of these is enough to justify 1:1 outreach on its own. They're filters, not triggers.

**The fix:** Layer signals. A "VP of Engineering at Series B fintech in California" is a list. A "VP of Engineering at Series B fintech in California who just posted about [topic] and has visited the site" is a play.

## Targeting Anti-Patterns

### Going around the AE of record
A BDR or marketer reaching out to a named account that already has an AE assigned — without coordinating — burns internal trust permanently. The AE finds out, the account gets confused, the deal dies, and the rep gets a reputation.

**The fix:** Always ask "who's the AE of record on this account?" before reaching out to a named account. Loop them in. If there's no AE, that's the answer.

### Pitching to the wrong persona
The buyer of your *category* isn't always the buyer of *your product*. A revenue ops leader buys revenue tools; a CFO doesn't. A devops engineer buys infrastructure; a CIO signs contracts but doesn't choose. Senior reps know which level is the chooser vs. the budget-holder.

**The fix:** For each play, identify the chooser (technical/operational owner) and the budget-holder. Address them differently — choosers get use-case detail, budget-holders get business outcomes.

### Pretending a prospect is a customer
"As a [company] customer, you've probably noticed..." when they're not a customer. Reps do this thinking it's a warm framing. It reads as either a mistake or a manipulation.

**The fix:** Don't blur the line. Either they're a customer or they're not. Address the actual relationship.

## Tone Anti-Patterns

### Performed urgency
"Closing this out by EOD." "Quick question." "Last chance." Manufactured urgency without a real reason behind it. Buyers see through it instantly. It signals desperation.

**The fix:** If there's real urgency (window closing, capacity filling, contract terms expiring), name the specific reason. If there isn't, drop the urgency framing.

### Surveillance creep
"I noticed you visited our pricing page on Tuesday at 3:42 PM and spent 47 seconds on the Enterprise tier." Even when accurate, the specificity reads as creepy. Buyers don't like to think about how much you can see.

**The fix:** Reference the *situation* in general terms. "Saw some interest in [topic] from your team" is the right level. Specifics about what they did, when, and for how long is too much.

### Hostage-style follow-ups
"I'll assume you're not interested if I don't hear back." "Should I close this out?" Variants of this are everywhere and they all feel coercive. They produce response rates short-term and burn the relationship long-term.

**The fix:** A clean break is better. "Wrapping this up unless I hear back" is fine. The "assume you're not interested" framing puts the buyer on the defensive. Don't do it.

### Passive-aggressive no-show emails
"Sorry I wasn't important enough to get on your calendar." "I see we missed our meeting — though clearly it wasn't a priority." These almost always come from frustrated reps and they always fail.

**The fix:** Neutral tone. "Didn't see you on the call — assuming something came up. Easiest path to reschedule: [link]." Move on.

### Over-personalization theater
"I noticed you went to [school] and have a dog named [name] and you commented on a post about [topic] last Tuesday." The rep is showing off their research, not connecting. Buyers find it off-putting.

**The fix:** One specific, relevant detail. The most important detail. Not three. The work is in *what to leave out*, not what to put in.

## Volume Anti-Patterns

### Touching twice in one week with two framings
The lookalike list email Monday, the funding round email Wednesday, the content download email Friday — all to the same account from the same rep. The buyer notices. They flag the rep as a spammer. The account is now harder to break into for the next year.

**The fix:** One framing per account per 14-day window. If a new signal comes in, decide whether the new signal is stronger than the previous one and which framing to lead with — but don't run them in parallel.

### Sequence past the point of disengagement
A buyer who hasn't responded to 5 touches over 21 days is telling you something. Continuing to send touches 6 through 12 doesn't reach them; it just confirms you're a vendor who doesn't respect their inbox.

**The fix:** Every play should have a stop condition. Stop. Move to a 90-day reminder. The signal will either come back or it won't.

### Recycling old leads with the same message
The lead didn't respond in March. The rep recycles them in June with a slightly tweaked version of the same email. Then in September. Then December. The lead is now poisoned — they associate the rep's name with low-effort outreach.

**The fix:** Never recycle a lead without a new hook. A new product, news at their company, a role change, a market shift. If you can't find a new hook, don't recycle yet.

## Channel Anti-Patterns

### Wrong channel for the relationship
Cold call to a junior IC at an enterprise. Physical mail to a $500 ARR account. LinkedIn DM that opens with a 3-paragraph pitch. Each channel has a register; mismatching the register fails.

**The fix:** Match channel intensity to relationship temperature. Cold = email + LinkedIn ads. Warm = email + LinkedIn DM. Hot = call + email. Existing customer = direct call/email/in-product.

### Channel pile-on
Email, LinkedIn DM, LinkedIn voice memo, calendar invite, follow-up text, and a phone call — all in 48 hours. The rep thinks it's persistence. The buyer experiences it as harassment.

**The fix:** Multi-channel is good. Multi-channel within 48 hours is bad. Space the touches across days, not hours.

### Forcing a meeting when async would work
Buyer asks a specific question via email. Rep responds with "let's grab 30 minutes to talk through it." The buyer wanted an answer, not a meeting. Forcing a meeting frame when the buyer prefers async signals you don't respect their time.

**The fix:** If the question can be answered in writing, answer it in writing. Offer a meeting as an option, not the path. "Happy to answer here or take 15 minutes — your call."

## Stage Anti-Patterns

### Running pre-renewal plays at month 11
The renewal motion that starts at 30 days out is already late. By then procurement has timed out, the budget cycle has closed, and the customer has had 11 months to silently drift.

**The fix:** Renewal motions start at 90 days minimum. The at-risk survey runs at 90-120 days. The terms conversation runs at 60. Renewal paperwork at 45. Active follow-up at 30.

### Multi-threading too early
Bringing a second persona into a deal that's still in discovery — when you don't yet have a clear champion — fragments the conversation. Now you have two confused buyers instead of one engaged champion.

**The fix:** Establish the champion first. Multi-thread when you have a clear champion who can sponsor the additional contacts.

### Onboarding light-touch on SMB
"They're small, we don't have the resources to onboard them properly." Then they churn at month 4. The unit economics still favor a real onboarding because the alternative is high churn.

**The fix:** Match onboarding intensity to LTV, not ACV. SMB onboarding is shorter and more automated, but it's not zero.

## Output Anti-Patterns (when running this skill)

### Producing the play description instead of the deliverable
The rep didn't ask for a description of how multi-threading works. They asked for the email to send. The output should be the email, not the explanation of the email.

**The fix:** Lead with the deliverable. The reasoning goes underneath as context, not on top as preamble.

### Generic email body ("Hi {first_name}")
Templates that look like templates produce template-level response rates.

**The fix:** Write the email as if to a specific person in a specific situation. Use placeholders only where the rep needs to fill in account-specific details — and mark them clearly.

### Single-touch outputs for plays that are multi-touch motions
Producing only the first email when the play is actually a 5-touch sequence over 14 days. The rep sends the first email, gets no response, and assumes the play failed. The play didn't fail — they didn't run it.

**The fix:** Always include the follow-up sequence and the stop condition. The rep should know the entire shape of the motion before they send the first touch.

### Forgetting to name the stop condition
The single most common omission in junior outreach plans. Without a stop condition, reps run sequences past disengagement and burn accounts.

**The fix:** Every play output ends with: "If [X] happens or [N] days pass with no response, stop."
